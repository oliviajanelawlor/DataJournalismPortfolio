Mental health services in the West Midlands:
“It felt like my brain was holding me hostage”


“I felt like I was being passed from pillar to post and not being listened to, given different medications and just told to ‘get on with it’,” Martha says.
The 22-year-old says she had been experiencing symptoms of depression and anxiety since the age of 14, but it wasn’t until university that she was given the help she needed.

Originally from South Wales, Martha says that before coming to the West Midlands, services and help felt virtually non-existent and the only help that she knew of for her age was Child and Adolescent Mental Health Services (CAMHS) and a school councillor:

“It felt like the lack of services and quality was making me so much worse, it felt like I just spiralled out of control.”


She wasn’t the only one.

A previous user of CAMHS, who wishes to remain anonymous, said that they too weren’t sure of what services were available as a teenager either:

“I was hoping to gain access to support, probably counselling services, by booking an appointment with my GP because I thought that’s what would be most helpful, as I was adamant I’d never take medication due to the stigma around it a few years ago.”

At the age of 13, they attended a service based in Walsall and was appointed an adolescent mental health practitioner, access to CAMHS and offered medication alongside these services:

“The mental health practitioner visited me weekly in school and taught me techniques to cope with my depression and anxiety. I was attended weekly counselling appointments by CAHMS, which was amazing, and I couldn’t have asked for someone better to help me through such a difficult time in my life.”

They refer to how their friends experienced a different situation, saying that they faced weeks of waiting times, switched between councillors and couldn’t find the “needed stability” they required for treatments.

Figures attained by the mental health charity Mind found that the demand for mental health services for children and young people had increased 17% in July 2019 from the previous year.

The demand for the service CAMHS was at its highest in July 2019 since August 2017 with 453,112 total appointments.



While the demand for the services rise, appointments cancelled by the NHS in England for CAMHS increased by 35% in July 2019 from the previous year.



After starting university and engaging in mental health services in the West Midlands, Martha started to feel like she was being “passed pillar to post.

“It took three years of going back and forth, along with me reaching extremely low points, self-harming and serious thoughts of suicide before I got the help I needed”.

The latest data from 2014 by the Office for National Statistics show that self-harm is most common among younger people in the age category 16–24 compared to other age groups.


Samaritans say:

“Self-harm is a sign of serious emotional distress and, while most people who self-harm will not go on to take their own life, it is a strong risk factor for future suicide.
From experience, Martha says:

“There should be more services that deal with ‘less serious’ cases as these can turn very serious.

“I shouldn’t have had someone on the crisis team tell me I needed to be trying to end my life there and then to get help. I couldn’t get out of bed, it felt like my brain was holding me hostage. I felt very isolated and felt like I wasn’t cared for.”


Details on where to find support in the West Midlands can be found on Mind Birmingham’s emergency contacts page.
